A Broad Margin
by Chris McPhee

About

I’m Chris McPhee. I’m a designer, a musician, a father of two, and a person who has owned this domain since June 1, 2013 without publishing a single word on it until twelve years later.

I live in Ottawa, Ontario. I lead design teams for a living and make synthesizers with my brother for fun. I have ADHD that went undiagnosed for most of my life, which means I’ve spent decades building elaborate systems to manage the gap between what I intend to do and what I actually do. This turns out to be more interesting than it sounds.

What this is

A Broad Margin is a stream. Essays, observations, links, photos, things I’m listening to — all flowing in one place. Some posts are long and considered. Some are a sentence. There’s no schedule, no newsletter cadence, no content strategy. Just whatever I’m thinking about, made visible.

The topics tend to orbit a few things: how we think, how we design the tools that shape our thinking, what it means to have a brain that works differently, and the slow process of actually doing the things you’ve been meaning to do for years.

If there’s a throughline, it’s this: the divergent mind needs convergent systems. I generate ideas constantly — connections, possibilities, projects, frameworks. Without some kind of structure to catch them, that energy dissipates. The creative work isn’t despite the need for systems. Building the systems is the creative work.

The name

“I love a broad margin to my life.”

Henry David Thoreau, Walden

Thoreau meant it literally — leave room. Don’t fill every hour. Don’t optimize every surface. Protect the space where nothing productive happens, because that’s where the unexpected thought arrives.

I’ve always loved that. The margin of a book is where the reader lives. It’s where your thumb goes, your annotation goes, your eye rests between sentences. The margin isn’t wasted space. It’s what makes the text readable. It’s what makes the whole thing breathe.

This site is my margin. The space I held open for twelve years without knowing what it was for. Turns out it was for this.
