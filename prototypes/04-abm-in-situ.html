Why Every Task App Is Broken

☞
Feb 14

I have a graveyard in my phone. Seventeen task apps, all of them beautiful, all of them built by thoughtful people who genuinely wanted to help me get things done. Todoist. Things 3. OmniFocus. Reminders. TickTick. Notion. Each one arrived like a new pair of running shoes â€” full of promise, laced with the conviction that this time the problem was the tool and not the feet.

Here's what none of them know about me: I have 47 unread reminders. I have swiped away more notifications than I've acted on. I have been told by a dozen onboarding flows to "just capture everything" and felt the familiar sinking when capturing everything became another thing I couldn't do. The apps aren't broken. They're just built for someone else's brain.

This essay is about what I learned when I stopped looking for the right app and started asking a more dangerous question: what does a task management system look like when it's designed for a brain that runs on interest, urgency, novelty, challenge, and passion â€” and literally cannot activate on importance alone?

The Ontological Problem

Every task app ships with a theory of how your mind works. That theory is almost never stated, but it's always there â€” embedded in the data model, the default views, the onboarding assumptions. And for most apps, the theory goes something like this:

  1. You know what needs to be done
  2. You can estimate when you'll do it
  3. Reminders at the right time will trigger action
  4. Completing tasks generates motivation to complete more tasks
  5. The gap between intention and action is primarily informational

Every one of these assumptions is wrong for an interest-based nervous system. Not slightly off. Architecturally wrong. It's like designing a house for someone who's told you they live in water. The foundation is the problem, not the fixtures.

The knowing-doing gap is not an information problem. You can know exactly what to do, know it matters, know the deadline is tomorrow, and still find yourself unable to start. This is not laziness. It is a nervous system that does not respond to importance as an activation signal.

I spent twenty years as a designer helping teams see the gap between their products' theory of reality and users' actual experience. The professional irony of doing that work while every tool I used to run my own life encoded a theory of my mind that was fundamentally wrong â€” well, it took a while to see it.

What the apps assume vs. what's actually true

The App Assumes What's Actually True
Tasks have stable priority Priority shifts based on interest, energy, and context
Due dates create urgency Everything is either "now" or "not now" â€” there is no gradient
Notifications trigger action Notifications trigger guilt, then dismissal
A clean inbox = a clear mind Maintaining the inbox becomes the task, replacing the actual tasks
Consistent daily review keeps things on track The system will decay in 2-6 weeks and that's normal

The divergent mind needs convergent systems. But the convergent systems on offer were designed for convergent minds.

The Graveyard Tour

Let me take you through the wreckage. Not to be morbid â€” to be diagnostic. Each failed app taught me something about what my brain actually needs, even as the app itself couldn't provide it.

Phase One: The GTD Era

I read Getting Things Done in 2008 and felt the particular euphoria of a framework that explains your whole life. David Allen's insight â€” that your brain is for having ideas, not holding them â€” hit like revelation. The problem is that GTD assumes a functioning executive function system for the weekly review, context switching, and project decomposition that makes the whole thing run. I could set up OmniFocus like a cathedral. I could not inhabit it past week three.

Phase Two: The Beautiful Minimalists

Things 3. Clear. Apple Reminders. The theory here was that complexity was the problem â€” if the tool is simple enough, you'll use it. But simplicity wasn't the problem. Activation was the problem. A beautiful empty list is still an empty list. I don't need fewer fields. I need a system that understands the difference between "I know I should do this" and "I can actually do this right now."

Phase Three: The Everything Tools

Notion. Obsidian. Roam Research. The theory flipped: maybe the problem is that tasks aren't connected to their context. If you can link everything to everything, the connections will carry you. And there's truth in this â€” for thinking. But tasks aren't thoughts. Tasks are commitments to act, and the commitment doesn't become easier because it's cross-referenced with a note from last Tuesday.

The pattern across all three phases: I was acquiring tools instead of inhabiting practices. Every setup was preparation. None of it was doing.

The key insight is the frontend/backend split. Active.md is the backend â€” every task with full metadata, context, energy level, project links. I never look at it. Focus.md is the frontend â€” a curated, glanceable view regenerated from the backend. It has eight sections: Today, This Week, Important & Slipping, Quick Wins, Deep Work Queue, Calls to Batch, Errands to Batch, and Practices to Remember.

The system runs on three principles:

  • Capture is dumb. Everything goes into today's daily note. No categorization at capture time. Sorting is a separate cognitive step and forcing it at the moment of capture kills the capture habit.
  • Processing is smart. A /process command routes captures to their destinations, infers metadata, regenerates the focus view. The intelligence is in the processing, not the input.
  • Focus is curated. I don't look at a list of everything. I look at a view designed for this moment â€” what's moveable, what's urgent, what matches my current energy.

The daily note as inbox

This was the breakthrough that made everything else work. There is no separate inbox. The daily note is the inbox. Everything I capture â€” a thought, a task, a link, a conversation summary â€” goes into today's daily note under a ## ðŸ”¥ Capture heading. That's it. One place. One gesture. The activation energy for capture drops to near zero because I'm already in the daily note for everything else.

☞

The design principle: reduce activation energy for the right actions to near zero. Make the path of least resistance also the path of greatest value. This is environment design applied to software.

Why plain text?

Because I've lost data to every proprietary format I've ever trusted. Because plain text files are the only format guaranteed to be readable in fifty years. Because markdown renders beautifully in Obsidian today and will render beautifully in whatever comes after Obsidian. Because the system serves me, not the other way around.

Technical details: the metadata schema

Each task in Active.md carries: status, energy level (low/medium/high), time estimate, project link, date added, date due (if real), and a context tag. The /process command infers most of these from the capture text and surrounding context. I correct when it's wrong, which is maybe 20% of the time. Good enough. Perfect is the enemy of done, especially for metadata.

The Deeper Pattern

Here's what I didn't expect: building the system taught me more about my brain than any self-help book or productivity framework ever did. Not because the system is clever, but because designing for yourself forces you to see yourself clearly. You can't build a task system for your brain without first understanding how your brain actually handles tasks. And that understanding â€” not the system â€” is the real product.

My father kept spiral notebooks. He wrote in them every day for decades â€” not because someone told him to journal, not because he'd read a book about it, but because his brain needed to externalize to function. He didn't have a word for it. I do. The word is access infrastructure. The notebook was his ramp to his own mind.

His father â€” my grandfather â€” walked. Long walks through the Scottish countryside, noticing things, saying nothing about them to anyone. Three generations of the same brain, finding three different containers for the same overflow.

What I'd Tell You

If you've tried every app and none of them stuck, the problem might not be discipline. It might be ontology. The app's model of your mind might be wrong. And if it's wrong, no amount of effort will make it right â€” you're running incompatible software on incompatible hardware.

⚠

This is not advice to build your own system from scratch. That's its own trap â€” the acquiring of system-building skills as a substitute for using them. Start with what you have. Pay attention to where it breaks. The breaks are data.

Three things I know for sure:

  1. Capture should be dumb and fast. If it takes more than one gesture, you won't do it when it matters. One inbox. No categorization at capture time. Sort later.
  2. The system will decay. Design for restart, not for permanence. Every system built for a divergent mind will be abandoned in 2-6 weeks. That's not failure. The only failure is not restarting.
  3. The most moveable thing beats the most important thing. When you're stuck, don't look for what matters most. Look for what you can actually do. Momentum transfers. Start anywhere.
If you want to try this yourself

You don't need Obsidian, though it's what I use. You need a folder, a daily file, and a focus file. Write today's date at the top. Dump everything there. At the end of the day, move the actionable things to the focus file. That's it. That's the whole system on day one. Everything else is refinement.

My father is gone now. I have some of his notebooks in a box in the basement. The handwriting is small and precise and completely unselfconscious â€” he never thought anyone would read them. He just needed to put the day somewhere outside his head so he could move through it.

I think about him every time I open my daily note. The tools are different. The impulse is the same. We externalize because our minds are too generative to hold everything internally, and the world wasn't designed for the way we process it. The notebook, the walk, the vault of markdown files â€” they're all the same gesture. They're all a hand reaching out to make a mark and saying: I was here. I noticed this. It mattered.

That's not a productivity system. That's a life practice.
